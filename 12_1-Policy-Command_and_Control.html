Today we start with the “command and control” policy approach.


Big picture, explain to me what a “command and control” policy does to solve an environmental problem?

  • (SLIDE)

Command-and-Control Regulations represent a regulatory approach to reducing pollution - e.g. Government tells people what they can/can’t do OR how they must do something


For Example:

  • You could put a ban on certain kinds of emissions (e.g. power plants may not release toxic mercury)

  • You could put a limitation on certain kinds of emissions (e.g. power plants may not release more than X pounds of toxic mercury)

  • You could mandate specific pollution-control technologies (e.g. power plants must install scrubbers that remove mercury from their emissions)


The Econ Textbook chapter I gave you argues that command-and-control approaches force polluters to pay the social costs of their pollution.

  • SLIDE: Let’s talk about the economics of regulating pollution!

Imagine a local billionaire buys some land and builds a factory to produce widgets he can sell for a profit.


As part of the widget production process the factory emits a byproduct that: - makes the river water undrinkable,

  • kills a local fishery, and

  • adds enough fine particulate matter to the air to increase asthma rates, heart attacks and reduce the productivity of local farms.


Why is this an example of pollution as an “externality”?


A market externality is “the effect of a market exchange on a third party who is outside or “external” to the exchange is called an externality” (OpenStax Link)


The primary exchange here is a company selling widgets to consumers

  • The pollution impacts a third party, separate from that exchange

  • In other words, it is not the purpose of the factory to produce pollution, that is external to its primary market purpose


In the absence of any laws against pollution, how many widgets should the factory owner produce?


Per Econ 101, it depends on two primary factors:

  1. How much demand is there for the widgets?

  2. How expensive are they to produce? (e.g. marginal cost per unit)


In other words, and in the absence of environmental laws, the factory should produce widgets to meet the demand while remaining sensitive to the cost


Make sense?

Time for a basic Econ 101 treatment of supply and demand

This blue line is what we refer to as the supply curve.


What does this “supply” line tell us about the production decision for this factory?

  • (The blue line represents the marginal cost per unit (MC) which tells you how much it costs to make one more widget at that level)

  • (The positive slope tells us that the cost per widget goes up as you produce more widgets)


Why is it so often true that supply curves have a positive slope?

- In other words, why does producing more widgets typically cost more money?

Let’s step through a hypothetical example to illustrate a positive sloped marginal cost curve.

Back to the local factory


Why can we probably assume increasing marginal costs for the widget factory?


The more widgets you want to make, the more materials, equipment, and staff you need to produce them

  • This is true for many, if not most, industrial production processes


Long story short, that’s the supply curve and why it typically has a positive slope.

  • As you make more widgets, you have to charge more for each of them or you won’t make a profit.


Make sense?


SLIDE: Alright, let’s talk demand which is the other half of this production function

Here’s the demand curve.


What do we learn about consumer demand for widgets from this negatively sloped line?

  • This indicates how many widgets people are willing to buy at different levels of the price.

  • Demand for widgets is price sensitive


SLIDE: For example

When the price is super high, few people are willing to buy the widgets.

  • For this factory, only 25 customers are willing to buy widgets at a $75 price point

However, as the price falls more customers are willing to purchase them

  • Here we see a price point of $20 finding customers for 80 widgets!


This can be a little confusing because the line is falling, but remember moving to the right means increasing the number of widgets made/sold.


Make sense?

In Econ 101 terms the factory owner should decide how many widgets to produce as a function of both curves

  • Here we see the marginal demand curve in red and the marginal cost curve in blue.

  • Where the two lines cross is the point of equilibrium


Why should the factory produce the number of widgets where the two lines cross each other?

- What is special about this equilibrium point?


Generally speaking, the equilibrium point maximizes the factory’s profit


More precisely, this is the last point at which you can increase production of new widgets and still earn more than they cost to make


Beyond this point, making more widgets would require you to lower the price to find a buyer and the marginal costs begin to exceed the returns at the lower price


SLIDE: Let me illustrate this visually for you

Here we see the sales for our factory at the equilibrium point

  • The red area represents the marginal costs of production (e.g. what you spend to make each widget in variable cost terms)

  • The blue area is the producer surplus (e.g. the sales at a level higher than what you need to break even)


SLIDE: In simple terms think of what each point along the supply line represents here.

For example, the supply curve shows us that the 10th widget you produce costs you about $20 to make

  • But in this case, you are selling that $20 widget for $43!

  • A definite win for you!

We can trace this logic along the line.

  • Here we see that the 35th widget we produce costs about $33, but again, we’re sellig it for the equilibrium price of $43.


At each point of production below the equilibrium we are selling the widgets for more than it cost us to make them


BUT, as we move up the supply curve line, the marginal cost increases and the incentive for us to make another widget shrinks.


Everybody understand the logic of the equilibrium point?

Let’s make sure we understand this basic intuition about the incredible power of the equilibrium point.


Why doesn’t a factory owner want to produce 85 widgets in the business scenario described by these two curves?

  • (The cost of production for the 85th widget FAR, FAR exceeds what you can get for them!)


SLIDE: Illustrate the logic

The red area represents the sum of our marginal costs for producing those 85 widgets.

  • That’s a lot of marginal cost!


Given these costs we’d need to sell our widgets for approximately $57 apiece


Per this demand curve, how many widgets can we sell at this price point ($57)?

This represents a market surplus and that’s really bad for the factory owner

Alright, let’s bring the environment back into this story.

Where does environmental harm exist on this hunt for equilibrium?

  • (SLIDE: It doesn’t!)

From this perspective, e.g. supply and demand, pollution is a problem of externality

  • It is a cost imposed on a third party outside the primary exchange of factory selling widgets to a consumer

  • Technically, these harms are separate to both the demand and the supply curves


This means that in a world with no environmental regulations the factory owner gets to make money and we all pay the costs of his/her business.

  • Hence our crazy polluted sky and water.


Anybody recognize this famous photo?


The Cuyahoga River in Ohio was once one of the most polluted rivers in the United States.

  • It has caught fire a total of 13 times dating back to 1868, including this blaze in 1952.

  • Yes, the RIVER was so full of crap being dumped into it by steel mills and other industry it was able to catch fire.

  • That probably shouldn’t happen.

Imagine the government decides to impose a command and control style regulation on the factory that limits its emissions into the air and water


How would that change the supply and demand lines on this plot?

  • (SLIDE: Raises the MC curve)

Here we see the effect of the regulation.

  • The marginal cost of producing each widget is increased to pay for the new processes required to be a cleaner factory

  • e.g. the cost of installing scrubbers on the smoke stack or cleaning the waste before dumping it into the river


Does this make sense?


What has this new regulation done to the market for widgets?

  • (SLIDE)

The equilibrium point has shifted to the left

  • The factory owner will now maximize profits by producing fewer widgets at a higher price, AND

  • Hopefully, each widget now produces fewer negative externalities than before


The benefit to society is that a command and control approach has the capacity to decrease the pollution immediately.

  • In essence, this regulation forces the factory owner to pay for the harms they are imposing on the rest of us.

  • Society still gets its widgets, but production of the widgets is now balanced against environmental harms.


And why can’t a factory owner simply produce the same number of widgets as before but sell them at the new higher price?

  • The market equilibrium point is self-enforcing!

  • If you produce more expensive widgets you can’t sell you will go bankrupt


Does this make sense?


Is this process of increasing marginal costs and reducing the number of widgets automatically a bad thing for the economy?

  • (SLIDE)

(Not necessarily!)


FIRST, a cleaner environment can have massive economic and health impacts - More people healthy, living longer, being more productive


SECOND, newer, cleaner technologies and refurbishing old buildings, etc. can boost employment! - You could absolutely employ more people than under the old system


Anyone who tells you this process is always bad or always good is not someone you should take seriously.

  • Many, many complicated feedbacks to consider…

A couple big picture takeaways we have to acknowledge when evaluating the CAA


Per the WHO, particulate matter (PM) is a common proxy indicator for air pollution.

  • It affects more people than any other pollutant.


The major components of PM are sulfate, nitrates, ammonia, sodium chloride, black carbon, mineral dust and water.

  • Chronic exposure to particles contributes to the risk of developing cardiovascular and respiratory diseases, as well as of lung cancer.

  • This means at high levels it tends to correlate with serious decreases in life expectancy.


WHO guidelines for 2021 break PM into coarse PM (< 10 microns) and fine PM (< 2.5 microns)

  • The guideline is to keep Fine PM below 15 micrograms and coarse PM below 45. (annual means)


The smallest circle here is 57 micrograms

  • So, in 1970 EVERY circle on this map FAR exceeds WHO danger thresholds.

  • And this is just where the monitoring was sufficient for reporting

50 years later and things look VERY different.

  • Note here that dark red, the wors level, is only 19.7 micrograms!

  • Still dangerous, but the 1970 map bottomed out at 57!!!!


The research has been fairly resounding that air quality standards have improved dramatically over time.

  • You can see impressive improvements since the 1970s in this plot.

Let’s end today with key takeaways.

ON BOARD


Pros:

  • Useful when the danger is most serious or time sensitive;
  • when the problem/solution is well understood;
  • when success demands compliance (flexibility is too risky),
  • Clear lines of oversight and mechanisms of enforcement.

Cons:

  • Often inspires backlash (huge stakeholder opposition),
  • government is often not great at “picking winners”,
  • offers no incentive to improve the quality of the environment beyond the standard set by a particular law,
  • is inflexible,
  • written by legislators and the EPA, and so they are subject to compromises in the political process,
  • Almost certainly inefficient (slow to implement, slow to adapt, inconsistently enforced)
