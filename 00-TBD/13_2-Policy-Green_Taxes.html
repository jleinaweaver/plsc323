Last week we explored command and control regulations.


In broad strokes, what does it mean to use C&C regulations to address an environmental problem?

  • (Create rules that alter the behavior of specific actors)

  • Examples:

    • Firms/Consumers must use technology X, OR

    • Firms/Consumers cannot emit more than a set limit of pollution Y


In Econ 101 speak, per the textbook: C&C means using rules to force polluters to adapt their production functions to the social costs of pollution

  • SLIDE: What does that mean? Back to the picture!

What does the supply line represent and why is it increasing?

  • (Marginal costs e.g. amount it costs to produce one more widget)

  • (Assumes marginal costs increase with quantity of product produced, e.g. sourcing more materials for your factory or building new equipment to extract oil from harder to work places, etc.)


What does the demand line represent and why is it decreasing?

  • (Amount of widgets you can sell at a given price point)

  • (Only so many people want the product at all and (almost) everyone’s desire for it will be sensitive to the price you charge for it.)


As the owner of the factory how many widgets should I make?

  • (SLIDE)

The equilibrium point, where the lines cross, maximizes your profit!

  • The point where you balance the marginal costs of production against the demand of your customers at a given price point


Why shouldn’t I, the factory owner, simply make more widgets in order to boost my profits?

  • (SLIDE)

Marginal cost per widget goes up SO you have to charge more for each of them, BUT consumers don’t want your widgets at those prices

  • You are now losing money (in ever growing amounts) for every extra widget you produce

  • So, push up the line and go bankrupt!


And why shouldn’t I, the factory owner, reduce the number of widgets I make in order to take advantage of the very high demand for a small number of widgets?

  • (SLIDE)

  1. (You’re leaving profit on the table, and)

  2. (You’re daring other companies to come into your market in order to meet the consumer demand!)


The key for us here is to acknowledge how powerful the equilibrium point is for determining the behavior of the factory!

  • The equilibrium point will be somewhat self-enforcing!


Now then, what happens if we add a C&C regulation requiring new, cleaner technology be added to the factory?

  • (SLIDE)

Rules that mandate emissions limits or the specific technology a factory has to use increases the marginal costs of production.

  • This shifts the equilibrium point to the left

  • The results: The factory will produce fewer widgets, the price for widgets goes up and the amount of pollution created goes down


And remember, this is not always a net negative for the economy!

  • Regulation design matters!

  • Higher costs can inspire innovation and efficiency

    • New products can come online to replace the older, dirtier widgets

  • Higher costs and unmet demand can inspire competition


Any questions on the basics of the approach?

Last week we explored this approach to environmental problem-solving using case studies, opinion pieces and reviews of empirical research.

Bottom line, what do we, as policy-designers, need to keep in mind if we chose to address an environmental problem with command and control type regulations?


  • Piggy-back on established institutions for support in oversight and enforcement

  • Useful especially if the problem is particularly dangerous or time horizons are VERY long

  • Be careful of economy wide effects (overall effect on the economy is hard to predict, could grow or shrink depending on many other factors involved)

    • Must be paired with assistance for impacted workers?

  • Be careful of political interests in the design (lobbying and/or protection of well connected industries)

  • Regulations have got to keep up with changes in the market and technology (no drift!)

  • Depends on balance of “dirty” vs “clean” industries in the target economy

  • C&C most realistic if changes are small OR industries carefully targeted?

  • Enforcement at the individual level is hard if community doesn’t buy-in

Policy Design: “Green” Taxes

This week we shift to your next policy design option: “Green” taxation policies (12.3 Market-Oriented Environmental Tools (OpenStax 2016))


Let’s talk textbook treatment of the approach first.

  • Note that the textbook chapter also explores marketable permits and property rights as other ways to use the market to address pollution.

  • We’ll focus this week just on the tax piece: “Pollution Charges”


In super broad strokes, what does it mean to use a tax to address an environmental problem?

  • (Apply a charge to some behavior (emitting pollution) forcing the polluter to consider cutting back on those emissions)

  • (Instead of mandating a specific technology or emissions limit with a rule, use a tax to make some of the costs of the pollution you create part of your production function)


Like last week, let’s walk through some of the key elements of this mechanism before we dig into the political arguments about it.

Let’s root ourselves back in the logic of how a C&C regulation increases the marginal costs of production.

  • The key here is that our C&C regulations are essentially treating all factories the same way

  • Every polluter must limit their emissions or adopt a new technology (e.g. smokestack scrubber X)


HOWEVER, it turns out that:

  1. Not every factory has the same slope on their supply curve, and

  2. It can be really hard for the government to know what the “right” regulation should be


SLIDE: Let’s now try thinking about factories as engines of pollution

In a sense, we don’t actually care how many widgets a factory makes.

  • Our interest is in how much pollution they make and what it would cost to reduce it

Who in society also pays the costs of the pollution produced by this factory?

Can anybody tell us what this means? How to read this line?


The marginal external cost of the pollution estimates the harms done by the factory’s pollution - e.g. smog leading to more asthma cases and heart attacks, river pollution killing fisheries, etc


Why is the slope of the line positive?

  • The cost is assumed to rise as emissions increase

  • A tiny bit of pollution is hardly noticeable, but as it increases so too do the harms


SLIDE: Example

Example: The 25th unit of pollution does $25 worth of harm to society

  • Small harms for small amounts of pollution

Example: the 75th unit of pollution does $75 worth of harm to society

  • Big pollution = Big harms


Make sense?

So, these two lines represent the pollution problem from both the factory and societies’ perspectives.


You will not be shocked to discover that many economists argue our goal should be to reduce pollution to the equilibrium point between these two lines!


SLIDE: Let’s examine how a green tax works as a way to understand the argument in favor of the equilibrium

The Basics of Taxing Pollution

Back to our starting point: A factory producing at the equilibrium between supply and demand.


Who is currently paying the costs for this factory’s pollution?

  • (Society is paying the cost for ALL of the factory’s pollution up to the 100th ton at a cost of $100)

  • The factory is paying nothing for dealing with its pollution

  • The definition of a negative externality


SLIDE: And now the tax!

Imagine we place a $25 tax on EVERY ton of pollution produced by this factory.

  • Here I’m focusing just on the effect of the tax on the factory as determined by its marginal cost of abatement.


Remember, we’re beginning at our baseline which is that the factory is emitting 100 units of pollution.

  • Thinking through the effect of this tax requires us to remember that these are MARGINAL cost curves

  • e.g. the cost to reduce each one unit of pollution


So, according to this marginal cost of abatement curve, how much does it cost for the factory to reduce its pollution by 1 unit (e.g. move from 100 to 99 units)?

  • (SLIDE: $1)

Hard to see, but a decrease of this 1 unit of pollution costs the factory $1


Focused just on the 100th unit of pollution, if you’re the factory owner would you rather pay the $25 tax or spend $1 to make your factory cleaner?

  • (Cleaning up your act is cheaper at the 100th unit)

Humor me and let’s keep tracking the logic.


As we move up the curve, cutting pollution gets more expensive.


Would you rather pay the government $25 to emit the 90th unit of pollution or spend $10 to make your factory cleaner?

  • (SLIDE)

We can follow this logic to this point.

  • Up to this point it is cheaper to improve your factory rather than pay the tax


However, beyond this point the trade-off flips

  • In order to cut your pollution from 75 units to 74 units presents a choice of either paying the government $25 or spending $26 to clean up your factory more.

  • From this point forward the tax is cheaper than improving your factory and we expect the cuts to stop


Everybody with me on this?


SLIDE: Let’s now switch to the other line and think about how much benefit society gets from cutting pollution at the extreme end

Here I’m showing you how the factory’s pollution level determines the harms done to society.

  • As we noted previously, we’re starting from a baseline production level of 100 units of pollution


So, to be clear, how much benefit does society receive from the factory reducing pollution by only 1 unit? (e.g. move from 100 down to 99 units)

  • (SLIDE)

Total Marginal Costs to Society

Area of a triangle = (base * height)/2

A 1 unit decrease at the highest level is worth $100!


Remember this is a marginal cost curve

  • Society pays $100 for the 100th unit and $99 for the 99th unit

  • If we don’t have the 100th unit of pollution, we don’t pay the extra $100 of costs


This is the direct reverse of what the factory faces at this end of the curve

Does that make sense?


SLIDE: Ok, let’s put this all together

This presents the societal trade-off in stark terms


At the extreme end of the pollution curve the choice is a simple one

  • The factory can cut the 100th unit of its pollution down to 99 units for only $1

  • Doing that removes the 100th unit of pollution which currently imposes $100 in harms on our community!

  • That’s a trade of $1 for the factory to get $100 for society!


Make sense?

That brings us full circle to the result of a $25 “green” tax operating on the factory

  • At this level the factory emits 75 units of pollution and society pays $75 in costs for that final unit of pollution


Why is this a worse outcome for society than pushing both dots to the equlibrium point?

- In other words, why is a $25 pollution tax not big enough?


You can see it clearly in the costs on the y-axis

  • At the 75th unit of pollution the factory pays $25 BUT society pays $75!

  • In other words, this tax treats the factory as THREE times more valuable than society


This set-up encourages us to think about how every widget the factory produces is actually paid for BOTH by society AND by the factory!

  • This means not applying a tax or setting a very low tax is explicitly deciding that society matters less than a widget factory.


Does everybody understand this intuition?


SLIDE: Let’s take it too far!

The $25 pollution tax leaves society accepting 3x the harms of the benefits being created.

  • So, we impose a $75 per unit tax on pollution and cut 75 units of pollution!


At each step from 100 units of pollution down to 25 the cost of improving your factory is less than the tax

  • So you invest in your factory and society gets less pollution.

Why would economists argue that this is equally “wrong” as a policy outcome?

- Why does the market wisdom object to this balance of costs and benefits?


We’ve reversed the imbalance, but haven’t necessarily made things any better!

  • Remember, the production of the factory is needed for the local economy to function!
    • The factory means jobs, growth, tax dollars for public goods, etc.
  • In terms of economic efficiency, the factory is now spending WAY more on harm reduction than society will benefit.
    • At this level of pollution tax the factory is paying $3 in costs to provide society only $1 in benefit!


Does this make sense?


(SLIDE: The “market” wisdom solution = A Pigovian tax)

Early 20th century British economist Arthur Pigou proposed a tax focused on the equilibrium point between these two curves.

  • A Pigovian tax reduces the externality to the point where the marginal costs to society equals the marginal costs to the factory.

  • Anywhere to the right of that point, the harms to society outweigh the benefits to the factory.

  • Anywhere to the left of the intersection, it would cost more to eliminate a ton of emissions than the harm that additional ton would cause.


Pigou’s aim was achieving an “efficient” economic outcome when determining the “optimal” pollution level in our community.

SLIDE: So, green taxes are more flexible, more efficient and…

Designing a tax means using the power of the market to reduce pollution.


Markets move MUCH faster and more efficiently to produce profits than top-down regulators possibly can.

  • The key is that the tax makes finding a way to pollute less during production a profit-maximizing decision.

  • If you make “being green” profitable then the market will take care of it for you.


Regulations are hard to design and are inflexible by design

  • Everyone must hit a certain cap or use a specific technology

  • While the cap can inspire innovation, technology standards rarely do
