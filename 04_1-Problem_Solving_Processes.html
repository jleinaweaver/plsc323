The DOC’S KEY Approach (Hughes 2007)

  • Definition
  • Objectives
  • Constraints
  • Strategies
  • Keepers
  • Experiment
  • Yes!

Here’s the Hughes (2007) approach he has coined as DOC’S KEY

  • It is, essentially, a seven step process for problem-solving


One of the elements I like a great deal from this book is Hughes discussion of constructing a problem definition

  • What is Hughes advice for us about constructing a definition? (e.g. the “D” in DOC’S KEY)


  1. He suggests there are three types of problem and that “unstructured” is the hardest to solve

    • “right-wrong problem” (e.g. math problems, puzzles, standardized test questions)
    • “rule-laden problem” (e.g. problem-solver bound by established rules or procedures; legal problems, recipes, tax prep)
    • “unstructured problems” (e.g. no right or wrong answer and no established rules to follow)

  2. Some really important concerns raised here about not confusing problems with strategies

    • Avoid letting strategies be presented as the problem
    • Problems are neutral and don’t typically need ‘must’ or ‘should’

SLIDE: Sum it all up in a process

Engaging Productively as Problem-Solvers

  1. Describe the problem facing our community

  2. Investigate the relevant stakeholders

  3. Frame the problem

  4. Design a policy to address the problem

  5. Take action to push the plan forward!

My general advice for community problem-solving goes something like this:


REVEAL: Step 1 focuses on establishing a baseline on the problem

  • Who or what is paying the costs?

  • What is the scientific basis of this problem?

  • What brought this problem to your attention?


REVEAL: Step 2 asks you to investigate the stakeholders

  • How do they define the basic concepts in play?

  • What are their preferred sources of wisdom or accountability?

  • Fundamentally speaking, do they already see this as a problem?


REVEAL: Step 3 asks you to develop a problem framing that will draw in as many stakeholders as possible.

  • Framing needs to present your problem in the most appealing light, AND

  • Make clear that this is a public problem requiring a collective decision

SLIDE: Let’s talk strengths and weaknesses of this general approach

Engaging Productively as Problem-Solvers

  1. Describe the problem facing our community

  2. Investigate the relevant stakeholders

  3. Frame the problem

  4. Design a policy to address the problem

  5. Take action to push the plan forward!

Important to recognize that all plans involve trade-offs

  • GROUPS, what are the strengths and weaknesses of adopting this approach to community problem solving?


Strengths:

  • Empowers individuals to tackle real-world problems (e.g. are you willing to do all this work then I think you can make progress!)

  • This process is HIGHLY adaptable to the context of a given problem

  • Achieving change in society is hard, so I want a process that reinforces and incentivizes the small steps necessary to achieve great things

    • Pushing the proverbial ball forward, even by just one of these steps is a VERY useful outcome for moving towards meaningful change


Weaknesses:

  • May limit your role in defining the “good” or normative objectives, makes you a facilitator of action rather than a definer of the end goal

  • Assumes you can identify the key stakeholders and that they are open to working with you on this “problem”

  • Very focused on smaller problems (by # impacted, # involved, area impacted, etc)
