SLIDE: One other important baseline we need for working on policy

EVERY policy creates winners and losers

Never forget, however, that in real-world problem-solving we play with live ammunition (so to speak)


Policy designers must NEVER forget that all policies create winners and losers.

  • Policy-making is ALWAYS a choice and all choices have consequences.


For example, Missouri just raised its minimum wage

  • Who benefits from increases to the minimum wage?

  • Who pays the costs for those increases?

  • Is there any way to pay workers more without distributing the costs to society?


The same is true with environmental policies

  • Take the clean air standards set by the Clean Air Act and the EPA

  • Who benefits from cleaner air?

  • And who pays the costs for it?

  • Is there any way to get cleaner air without impacting polluting industries?


Humor me, can we think of any policy or rule in our society whether implemented by government, school, or your employer, that does not impose costs on someone?


So, policy is, in essence, a rule or rules adopted by a society to structure behavior.

  • All rules impose costs on some and provide benefits to others

  • Policy-making is often an exercise in deciding who will pay the costs and who will receive the benefits.


Ok, so:

  1. Government is the label we apply to efforts by human societies to provide for the needs of their own community, and

  2. EVERY policy is designed to help someone but inevitably hurts someone else


Then the question must become, why can’t we get the positive ends without the negatives

SLIDE: Let’s now shift to the next important piece of the Munger chapter and that is thinking about the policy-making process

Problem-solving in a community requires policy, but policymaking has pre-requisites!

Just because you now accept that a problem is serious and needs policy to address it, doesn’t mean the community is on board!

  • In Figure 2.3 Munger lays out his “three stages of policy choice”

  • aka “Deciding How to Decide”


In essence, Munger’s three steps are:

  1. Convince the community that there is a problem,

  2. Convince the community that it should be addressed collectively, and

  3. Convince the community of how the specific policy should be adopted


Fundamentally, this figure is a reminder for ALL policymakers: BEFORE you try to solve ANY problem, you MUST make a number of convincing arguments


SLIDE: For our purposes, I’m going to boil these down to two distinct steps I’m describing as the pre-requisites of policy

Policymaking Pre-Requisites


You must convince the community that:

  1. We have a public problem, and

  2. We require a collective decision to solve it

Each pre-requisite is a distinct argument that you have to successfully make before moving to any actual policy-design


In other words, before we can debate rules, new taxes, subsidies or anything else you first must succeed in convincing the community that collective action is needed.

  • This is NOT an easy step, but the better you do this the easier the rest of the process becomes.


Let’s start with the first of these

SLIDE: Markets

Munger’s (2000) Three “Wisdoms”

  1. Markets

  2. Politics

  3. Experts

What would it mean to adopt the markets as your primary “wisdom” for policymaking?

  • In other words, how might a markets-focus be used to solve environmental problems?


Preference for allocating resources AND solving problems using systems of private exchange.

  • Concerned primarily with process, less so with outcomes

  • Preference for non-interventionist style policies wherever possible

  • Rules only to keep that market well functioning (contract enforcement, etc)


What would a market-based solution to our magical fishery resource look like?

  • How many fishers? Who fishes? What about enforcement?


According to the chapter, what are the pros and cons of prioritizing a markets focus over the others?

Pros:

  • Most efficient by definition (most bang for the resource buck)


Cons - Market failures such as:

  1. Monopolies (e.g. antitrust),

  2. Externalities (e.g. pollution),

  3. Under supply of public goods (e.g. free-riding), and

  4. Information problems (e.g. drug safety); Reduces all problems to maximization of use issues

Munger’s (2000) Three “Wisdoms”
