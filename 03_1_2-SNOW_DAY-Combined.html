Problem-solving in a community requires policy

Let’s refresh some of the big arguments from our discussions


First and foremost, what is “policy”?

  • (SLIDE: The Burke quote)

I like the Burke quote just because it puts the focus in the right place

  • Government is a human effort to deliver what we want

  • Policy is, in essence, rules created by a government or community for managing our needs


According to Munger, why is policy a non-optional requirement for all communities?

  • Why can’t we just leave each other alone?

  • (SLIDE: The Hun-gats thought experiment)

Munger described the Hun-gats tribe as a pre-historic human community that was running out of food

  • The tribe lacks information on what new foods are safe to eat and what are not

  • Survival odds are maximized by coordinating our experimentation on food!

  • Every man for himself leads to rampant death through wasted experimentation


Bottom line:

  1. Policy is not optional in a community if you want to survive

  2. There was never a “state of nature”

    • Stop pretending your intuitions about what people would do without government matter for how we should govern ourselves

  3. Battles over expertise are unavoidable and a key part of the process!

    • There is no “perfect” policy, all policies create winners and losers


SLIDE: If there is no perfect policy, what do we aim for?

To maximize your chances of success, your policy proposals must be:

  • Specific,

  • Adapted to the stakeholders,

  • Adapted to the science of the problem,

  • Adapted to the rules of the community, and

  • Be at least as good as the alternatives.

Read slide


I’m not saying you can’t create rules that violate these characteristics, but you shouldn’t if the goal is to produce durable and positive change in a community!


SLIDE: And that took us to the SUPER important pre-requisites for all public policy

Policymaking Pre-Requisites


You must convince the community that:

  1. We have a public problem, and

  2. We require a collective decision to solve it

Each pre-requisite is a distinct argument that you have to successfully make before moving to any actual policy-design


What does it mean to say we have a public problem? As opposed to what?

  • (Private Problem: “My choice has no consequence for your welfare”)

  • (Public Problem: “My choices affect your welfare”


What does it mean to say we require a collective decision? As opposed to what?

  • (Individual Decision: “I can choose, alone and without interference”)

  • (Collective Decision: “Choices are made by a group, and are binding on all”)

Theory as a Scientific Tool


Ok, put that characteristic in this diagram in place of the word “predictor”

  • e.g. that is the variable we are suggesting causes variation in policy outcomes

  • The arrow represents the direction of causality

  • Here variations in the predictor cause variations in the outcome

Your explanation why (e.g. the story behind this arrow) IS your theory

  • Theories, in science, are the stories we have designed to help us explain the world

  • They are simplifications of complex processes meant to help us understand why things happen the way that they do
